Few-Shot Multi-Disease Blood Smear Diagnosis

Learning From Less — diagnostic classification under severe label scarcity

Oni, S. · ML Collective · 2024–2025
Few-Shot Learning Medical Imaging Clinical AI PyTorch

Abstract

Clinical machine learning in low-resource settings runs into a structural problem before it runs into any technical one: labelled medical imagery is scarce, and the diseases that most need it are often the ones with the least curated data. We built a few-shot classifier that diagnoses sickle cell anemia, malaria, leukemia, and trypanosomiasis from blood smear images, trained under deliberately constrained label budgets. The model was deployed for real-time inference on unseen samples with class-level prediction output. Awarded Best Poster at Deep Learning Indaba 2025.

Motivation

The four diseases targeted by this work — sickle cell anemia, malaria, leukemia, and trypanosomiasis — share two properties: they are diagnosable from blood smear microscopy, and they disproportionately affect populations where labelled training data is scarce. The standard ML response is to assemble a larger labelled dataset. The clinical response is to make do with what's available.

Few-shot learning sits between those two responses: rather than requiring thousands of labelled examples per class, it asks whether a model can generalize from tens. For a clinic in a low-resource setting, that's the difference between a useful tool and a research curiosity.

Methodology

Data

We assembled a curated set of blood smear images spanning the four target conditions, deliberately keeping the per-class label count low to mirror the scarcity that motivated the work. Image quality was uneven by design — clinical microscopy doesn't produce uniform images, and a model trained only on pristine inputs would not survive deployment.

Architecture & training

We used a few-shot learning approach with metric-based generalization, fine-tuned on the curated dataset under strict label budgets. The training objective optimized for class separability under low-shot conditions rather than headline accuracy on a large held-out set.

Real-time inference

The trained model was deployed for real-time inference on unseen blood smear samples, producing class-level predictions with confidence scores. The deployment loop was kept deliberately simple — a clinician should be able to use the output without reading the paper.

Findings

The classifier produced usable per-class predictions across all four conditions under the constrained label budget. Performance on unseen samples held up well enough to justify real-time inference. The clearest failure mode was on edge cases where smear preparation quality dropped sharply — a failure mode that mirrors what a junior clinician would also struggle with, which makes it a reasonable handoff point for human review rather than a silent error.

Why It Matters

Most clinical ML benchmarks report accuracy on large, well-curated test sets. Most clinics do not have large, well-curated training sets. The gap between those two facts is where deployment fails. This work was a small attempt at closing it — and the Best Poster recognition at DLI 2025 suggests the framing landed with the broader African ML community as well.

The ongoing question is how far few-shot methods can go before they hit a structural ceiling — and what hybrid approaches (few-shot + targeted active learning + clinician-in-the-loop) buy in real deployment.
