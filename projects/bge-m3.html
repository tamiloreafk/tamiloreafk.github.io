BGE-M3 for Nigerian Languages

Improving BGE-M3 Multilingual Dense Embeddings for Nigerian Low-Resource Languages

Omotoso, A., Shopeju, H., Joshua, A., & Oni, S. · 2025 · pp. 224–229
Multilingual NLP Dense Retrieval BGE-M3 Low-Resource Languages Information Retrieval

Abstract

Standard multilingual retrieval encoders cover dozens of high-resource languages well and Nigerian languages poorly. We fine-tuned BGE-M3 dense embeddings on three Nigerian languages for information retrieval over local document corpora and measured a 20% improvement in Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) over the baseline BGE-M3 across the three target languages. The work was published in the Proceedings of the 9th Widening NLP Workshop in Suzhou and awarded Best Poster at Deep Learning Indaba 2025.

Motivation

The multilingual NLP literature reports headline numbers that include "African languages" as a single bucket, but the bucket is heterogeneous. Hausa, Yoruba, and Igbo each have distinct morphology, tone systems, and orthographic conventions. A model that ranks documents acceptably for one will not necessarily rank them acceptably for another.

BGE-M3 is one of the strongest off-the-shelf multilingual dense encoders. We chose it as our starting point not because it's perfect for Nigerian languages, it isn't, but because closing the gap on a strong baseline is a more meaningful contribution than closing it on a weak one.

Methodology

Target languages & corpora

We targeted three Nigerian languages with sufficient digital corpora to support fine-tuning while remaining low-resource by any reasonable definition. The training corpora were assembled from local document sources, news, government publications, community-written content, to keep the domain distribution representative of how the model would actually be queried.

Fine-tuning protocol

BGE-M3's architecture supports dense, sparse, and multi-vector representations. We focused on the dense retrieval head, fine-tuning with contrastive objectives over query–document pairs constructed from the corpora. Hyperparameter sweeps were deliberately conservative: the goal was a reproducible, deployable encoder rather than a single squeezed leaderboard number.

Evaluation

We evaluated using Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) and nDCG over held-out query sets across all three target languages. The baseline was BGE-M3 with no fine-tuning. We reported per-language results to avoid the bucket-averaging problem the work was designed to push against.

Findings

Across the three target languages, fine-tuned BGE-M3 produced a consistent 20% improvement in MRR over baseline. The improvement held across query types, including the tonally-marked queries that the baseline handled most poorly. Where the baseline confused near-homographs that differed only by tone marking, the fine-tuned encoder consistently disambiguated them.

The clearest gains came on queries involving named entities specific to the corpora, the kind of vocabulary the global pre-training distribution doesn't see often. The encoder learned that vocabulary cleanly without forgetting general retrieval competence.

Why It Matters

Retrieval is upstream of nearly every applied NLP system, RAG, search, recommendation, agent grounding. If the retrieval layer fails for a language, every downstream system inherits the failure. The 20% MRR improvement isn't an end in itself; it's a demonstration that closing the gap is achievable with modest, deployable interventions on top of strong baselines.

The work was awarded Best Poster at Deep Learning Indaba 2025, Kigali, recognition that the problem framing resonated with the broader African ML research community.
